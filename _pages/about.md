The problem that's followed me through most of my career is some version of the same one: how do you build a system that can make sense of an uncertain world and act in it without being told exactly what to do? It showed up first in robotics, then in medical imaging, then in production ML, and lately in LLM agents. The domain keeps changing. The question doesn't.

I currently work at Dalux, applying machine learning, computer vision, and LLMs to problems in the construction industry. The work is production-grade: systems that have to hold up in real environments, with real users, and real consequences when they don't. That last part matters to me. A lot of what I think about isn't modeling. It's everything around the model: how the problem is framed, what failure modes you decide to care about, how the system behaves when things go wrong. That's usually where the interesting decisions live.

LLM agents have been taking up more of my thinking lately. Getting them to behave predictably, fail gracefully, and do something genuinely useful beyond demos turns out to be a harder problem than it looks, and the engineering patterns are still being worked out, which makes it interesting.

Before Dalux I did a PhD at DTU on AI for medical ultrasound: 3D reconstruction and machine learning applied to a domain that's noisy, operator-dependent, and full of clinical constraints that don't care about your validation metrics. The stakes clarify things. It's a useful place to have learned what actually matters in a system.

I came to all of this through electronics engineering and then autonomous systems at DTU, which is where the original pull toward robotics and physical systems started. That ambition hasn't gone away. It's just taken less embodied forms for now.
